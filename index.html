01 The Mix

Soft skills make up two-thirds of all skill demand.

While credential inflation once centred on formal degrees, the focus has pivoted to skills. In this new landscape, human-centric capabilities dominate market demand and act as the common language of the labour market.

02 Seniority

Technical skills lean senior; soft skills skew entry-level.

Driven by AI's capacity to automate junior tasks, the credential market has split into a clear talent bifurcation. High formal barriers remain strictly locked in place for scarce, senior-level technical roles, while entry-level positions have lowered requirements to focus heavily on foundational human capabilities.

The divide

Machine learning 80% · PyTorch 84% · NLP 83% senior. The deepest technical skills are almost never asked of early-career candidates.

Teamwork 64% · Interpersonal 60% · Communication 60% entry. Human-centric skills are the entry ticket.

This is the early-career aperture: the skills open to newcomers are precisely the human-centric ones.

03 Who's Hiring

Entry-level demand has trailed senior throughout the period.

In every single month from January 2021 to April 2026, total skill mentions in senior-tagged postings exceeded entry-level ones — and the gap widened from ~30K mentions/month at the start to roughly 100K by 2025-09. Hiring volume rose for both, but the early-career deficit held.

04 The Shift

Communication demand slips as AI skills climb.

As AI skills grow more capable of automating routine tasks and lifting on-the-job performance, employers increasingly screen for them — and that rising demand is steadily reshaping the skill mix away from a once-dominant reliance on communication alone.

AI-related skills rising ↑
while Communication's share eases ↓
Reading the trend

Communication's share of skilled postings eased from ~57% in 2021 to ~45% in 2026 — a 12-point drop. Meanwhile AI-related skills (AI, ML, Deep Learning) climbed from a near-zero base into double digits in postings that mention them. The mix is moving, not just the volume.

Caveat for the room: there is a fixed set of soft skills but an ever-growing list of hard skills. Some hard-skill rise reflects new tools entering the panel — not only deeper demand per job.

05 The Trend

Hard-skill demand has outpaced soft over time.

As employers drop degree requirements and lean on skills as the primary screening mechanism, demand for hard skills is accelerating at a relentless pace — outrunning soft-skill growth and resetting the bar candidates must clear.

06 Education

Soft skills are near-universal at every degree level; technical skills are not.

Soft-skill demand sits between 85% and 91% from High School through PhD — a six-point band. Hard-skill demand swings twelve points (61% to 73%) and peaks at the Bachelor's level, then falls again at Master's and PhD.

Stable across the ladder

Soft-skill demand sits between 85% and 91% from High School through PhD — a six-point band. Hard-skill demand swings twelve points (61% to 73%) and peaks at the Bachelor's level, then falls again at Master's and PhD.

Stable — Four of the five degree levels stay in a narrow 6-point band across the entire window. Soft-skill demand doesn't move with the cycle.

The Bachelor's break — Only the Bachelor's line diverges, and only in 2025. The level employers hire from most often saw the steepest soft-skill retreat.

07 Education Over Time

Soft-skill demand is structural, not cyclical — but the Bachelor's market just cracked.

High School, Associate, Master's and PhD postings all hold between 86% and 92% across five years, while Bachelor's postings fall from ~91% to ~78% in early 2025 before partially recovering — a 13-point break in the country's most-hired credential.

Two stories at once

Stable — Four of the five degree levels stay in a narrow 6-point band across the entire window. Soft-skill demand doesn't move with the cycle.

The Bachelor's break — Only the Bachelor's line diverges, and only in 2025. The level employers hire from most often saw the steepest soft-skill retreat.

The PhD line wiggles because monthly PhD sample sizes are small — read the level, not the noise.

08 The Money

The accessible soft-skill roles pay less — the premium is in the pairing.

Behavioural skills elevate technical labour into recognised professionalism, unlocking a wage premium. This financial bump reflects the price the market pays for cultural legibility, rewarding the professional identity a worker signals rather than just their technical execution.

The pairing premium
+$3.2K
+7.0% lift when soft-skill roles add hard skills

Soft-only postings average $46K and out-earn Hard-only ($35K), but Hard + Soft together reach $49K — a +$3.2K (+7.0%) lift over soft-only and +$14K over hard-only. The pairing, not either skill type alone, sets the salary ceiling.

The career message for early talent: pair your human-centric strengths with a technical edge.

09 Where It's Coming From

AI-skill demand is rising in every field — Marketing leads the gain.

AI skill requirements are rising across multiple industries, reflecting its growing role as a common workplace expectation. This shift is particularly notable in marketing, finance, and operations, which currently lead the steady increase.

Measured as share of a field's postings — within-job proportion, not raw volume Every field increased; none declined
The broadening

Marketing's gain — the largest of any field

Marketing postings asking for ≥1 AI-related skill rose +2.1 percentage points from 2021 to 2026 — the largest increase of any field. Finance (+1.3 pp) and Operations (+1.0 pp) follow; even Engineering (+0.6 pp) and Sales (+0.7 pp), the lowest movers, still moved. None declined.

This answers where the increase comes from: it's broad-based across fields, measured as each field's own share of postings — so it reflects genuine spread, not the growing skill list alone.
